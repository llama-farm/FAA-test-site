ACS Exam Weighting — Where to Focus Your Study Time

The FAA test draws questions based on these Airman Certification Standards (ACS) content area weights:

Regulations (UA.I)
48%
Operations (UA.V)
25%
Airspace & Requirements (UA.II)
20%
Weather (UA.III)
5%
Loading & Performance (UA.IV)
2%

Strategy: Spend most time on Regulations and Operations (73% of the test). Airspace is 20% and often chart-based. Weather and Loading are lower-weight but still appear.

Getting Started — IACRA & Your FTN

Before you take the FAA knowledge test, you need an FAA Tracking Number (FTN). Get one by creating a profile in IACRA:

  1. Go to IACRA (iacra.faa.gov)
  2. Click "Register" to create a new account (or sign in if returning)
  3. Complete your profile to get your FTN
  4. Your FTN will be printed on your Airman Knowledge Test Report (AKTR) after you pass
  5. After passing the UAG test, return to IACRA to complete your Remote Pilot Certificate application

Scheduling & Taking the Test

The UAG knowledge test is administered by PSI at testing centers nationwide. Here's how to sign up:

  1. Get your FTN first (see IACRA section above)
  2. Go to PSI Exams (faa.psiexams.com) to create an account and schedule your test
  3. Select "Unmanned Aircraft General (UAG)" as the exam type
  4. Choose a testing center and date (cost is approximately $175)
  5. Bring a valid government-issued photo ID to the testing center
  6. You may use an approved testing supplement (provided at the center) during the exam

Tip: PSI also offers a free online practice test. Try it before your real test to get comfortable with the interface.

    30 Key Facts to Memorize

    1
    The RPIC is the final authority. The remote pilot in command is directly responsible for and the final authority as to the operation. Not the owner, not the manufacturer, not the visual observer.
    2
    VLOS must be maintained at all times. Visual line of sight with unaided vision (other than corrective lenses). A visual observer assists but does NOT replace VLOS.
    3
    Yield to ALL aircraft. Small UAS must yield right of way to all aircraft and airborne vehicles. No exceptions.
    4
    One drone per pilot. You cannot operate more than one UA at the same time as RPIC, manipulator, or visual observer — regardless of automation.
    5
    No hazmat — and you can't waive it. Hazardous material carriage is prohibited and is the one rule that cannot be waived.
    6
    Night = anti-collision lights. Night and civil twilight ops require anti-collision lighting visible for 3 SM. Can be dimmed for safety, never extinguished.
    7
    ATC authorization: B, C, D, and E-surface. Prior ATC authorization is required for Class B, C, D, and Class E surface areas at airports. Class G = no auth needed.
    8
    Prohibited Areas = no entry. Restricted = call first. P-areas: no flight. R-areas: contact the controlling agency when active. MOAs: not prohibited, but use caution.
    9
    TFRs apply to all aircraft. A Temporary Flight Restriction applies to everyone. Check NOTAMs before every flight.
    10
    Chart altitudes are in MSL, not AGL. Shelf floors and ceilings on sectional charts are in MSL. Don't confuse airport elevation (MSL) with height above ground (AGL).
    11
    Unstable air = turbulence + cumuliform clouds + showery precip. Stable air = smooth air, stratiform clouds/fog, poor visibility in haze. This shows up on almost every weather question.
    12
    Temp close to dew point = fog likely. When temperature and dew point converge, the air is near saturation and fog or low cloud formation becomes likely.
    13
    High density altitude = bad performance. Hot + high elevation + humid = thinner air = propeller efficiency down, climb performance down, payload margin down.
    14
    Stall = exceeding critical angle of attack. A stall can happen at any airspeed. It's about angle of attack, not speed. Load factor increases stall speed.
    15
    IMSAFE: Illness, Medication, Stress, Alcohol, Fatigue, Emotion. Use this checklist before every flight to assess your fitness to fly.
    16
    PAVE: Pilot, Aircraft, enVironment, External pressures. Use PAVE to assess risk before a mission. External pressure traps are a favorite exam topic.
    17
    Know all 5 hazardous attitudes. Anti-authority, Impulsivity, Invulnerability, Macho, Resignation. Know the characteristic phrase AND antidote for each.
    18
    Risk Management breaks the accident chain. To prevent the "final link" in the accident chain, use Risk Management methodology.
    19
    Category 4 = airworthiness certificate. Only Category 4 requires an FAA airworthiness certificate. This is the most common "which category" trap.
    20
    The manufacturer submits the DoC, not the pilot. Declaration of Compliance is submitted by the manufacturer/designer/modifier. The pilot verifies and uses compliant aircraft.
    21
    Remote ID failure = land ASAP. If standard Remote ID stops broadcasting in flight, land as soon as practicable.
    22
    FRIA = for drones WITHOUT Remote ID only. FAA-Recognized Identification Areas are for drones without Remote ID equipment. Standard Remote ID drones can fly anywhere.
    23
    Category 3 cannot fly over open-air assemblies. Category 2 can (with DoC). Category 3 is more restricted and cannot operate over open-air assemblies of people.
    24
    Chart Supplement U.S. = most comprehensive airport info. Not NOTAMs, not the sectional chart, not the TAC. The Chart Supplement U.S. is the answer.
    25
    CRM = all phases, not just flight. Crew Resource Management must be integrated into planning, briefing, flight, and post-flight.
    26
    Ceiling = lowest BKN or OVC layer. FEW and SCT are not ceilings. Cloud bases in METARs are reported in hundreds of feet AGL.
    27
    METAR winds are in degrees TRUE. Wind direction in METARs is always reported in degrees true, not magnetic.
    28
    Emergency = RPIC can deviate from rules. During an in-flight emergency, the RPIC may deviate from Part 107 rules to the extent necessary. Report upon FAA request.
    29
    Latitude = N/S, Longitude = E/W. Latitude measures north and south of the equator. Longitude measures east and west of the Prime Meridian.
    30
    No manufacturer maintenance schedule? Create your own. If the manufacturer doesn't provide one, establish your own scheduled maintenance protocol.

    Common Exam Traps

    Confusing AGL and MSL on charts. Chart shelves are MSL. Your drone altimeter reads AGL. They are not the same.
    Thinking Class E always needs authorization. Only Class E surface areas at airports do. Not all Class E.
    Thinking a visual observer replaces VLOS. It doesn't. The VO assists, but the RPIC still must maintain VLOS.
    Believing you can fly two drones with automation. No. One drone per pilot, period.
    Forgetting anti-collision lights can be dimmed but not OFF. Safety requires dimming sometimes, but never extinguish.
    Mixing up who submits the DoC. The manufacturer submits it. The pilot uses compliant aircraft.
    Confusing 0.55 lb with the Part 107 threshold. 0.55 lb is for Category 1 over people and registration. 55 lb is the Part 107 max.
    Assuming Category 3 can fly over crowds. It can't. Only Categories 1 and 2 (and 4) can fly over open-air assemblies.
